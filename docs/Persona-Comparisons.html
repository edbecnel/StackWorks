Persona Comparisons

A reader-friendly comparison of Teacher, Balanced, Trickster, and Endgame bot personas across the opening, mid-game, and endgame phases of draughts and checkers play.

Opening Phase

In the opening phase, the bots set the tone for the entire match. Here is how each persona handles the first 5–10 moves of a draughts or checkers game:

Feature Teacher Bot Balanced Bot Trickster Bot Endgame Bot
Opening Priority Plays “Classical” lines (e.g., the Old Faithful or Apex). Aims for a solid “V” or “Pyramid” formation in the center. Plays “Dyke” or “Asymmetrical” lines to create gaps. Focuses on “The Move” (Opposition) counts early on.
Development Moves pieces from the back row slowly to show you the “safety” of the base. Develops both wings (Left/Right) equally to maintain board balance. Leaves its “Bridge” (the two middle back-row pieces) open to bait you. Keeps the back row strictly intact to prevent you from crowning early.
Trade Preference Avoids trades early to keep the board “busy” and instructional. Accepts standard 1-for-1 trades to keep the game “level.” Willingly offers a 1-for-1 trade if it results in an open lane for a “shot.” Only trades if it simplifies the board toward a winning 3-vs-2 endgame.
Positioning Stays away from the “side-alleys” (edges) to model good habits. Occupies the center to prevent you from splitting its forces. May move to the edges to lure you into “cramping” its position. Positions pieces to eventually “lock” your pieces against the side.
Pace of Play Slow and steady; focuses on building a “readable” wall. Moderate; responds to your moves with standard defensive theory. Aggressive; tries to force you into a “forced jump” situation by move 6. Methodical; treats the opening simply as a “setup” for the final 10 moves.

Opening Snapshot

  • Teacher: Moves a piece, then waits to see if you respond with the “correct” counter-move. It’s a textbook start.
  • Balanced: Creates a “diamond” shape in the middle. It’s hard to break through, but it’s not attacking you yet.
  • Trickster: Might purposefully create a “hole” in its second row, hoping you’ll jump into it and get trapped by its back-row pieces.
  • Endgame: Moves pieces in a way that minimizes your mobility. It wants to “shrink” the board as fast as possible.

Mid-Game Strategy

This comparison table illustrates how each persona would approach a typical mid-game scenario in a draughts-type game (e.g., American Checkers or International Draughts).

Feature Teacher Bot Balanced Bot Trickster Bot Endgame Bot
Primary Move Choice A safe, “book” move that reinforces a central cluster. A solid move that maintains balance between both flanks. A provocative move that leaves a piece seemingly “hanging.” A maneuvering move that prepares a path to the king row.
Reaction to Your Attack Retreats or blocks in a way that clearly shows you the defensive “wall.” Makes a 1-for-1 trade to neutralize the threat and simplify the board. Deliberately lets you jump a piece to pull you into a “double jump” trap. Defends stubbornly, prioritizing the protection of its back row.
Central Control Stays in the center to show you why it’s the strongest position. Fights for the center to avoid being pushed to the weaker edges. May abandon the center temporarily to lure your pieces into an “edge trap.” Uses the center as a launching pad to advance pieces toward promotion.
Sacrifice Logic Rarely sacrifices, unless it’s to demonstrate a basic “forced jump” rule. Only sacrifices for an immediate and equal piece-for-piece exchange. Constantly seeks “shots” where it gives 1 piece to capture 2 or 3. Will sacrifice a piece only if it guarantees a King or a winning “opposition.”
Handling Mistakes May ignore a winning tactic of its own to see if you spot your own error. Plays the “best” engine move for its level without being flashy. Punishes every single mistake you make with a rapid, flashy combination. Ignores small tactical skirmishes to focus on the long-term “win.”

Example Scenario: The “Bait”

Imagine you have a chance to jump one of the bot’s pieces near the edge:

  • Teacher might let you take it to show you that “edge pieces are weak.”
  • Balanced would likely have protected that piece with another one behind it.
  • Trickster wants you to take it because that jump lands your piece in a hole where it can jump three of yours.
  • Endgame would only let you take it if the trade helps it get a King faster on the other side of the board.

End-Game Strategy

In the endgame of draughts and checkers, the focus shifts from mass formations to the precision of a few pieces—specifically the power of Kings and the technical rule of “The Move” (Opposition).

Feature Teacher Bot Balanced Bot Trickster Bot Endgame Bot
Primary Focus Fundamental rules. It lets you practice basic king-trapping. Standard exchanges. It aims for a draw unless you make a major blunder. Baiting traps. It offers “free” pieces that lead to a sudden loss for you. Technical precision. It uses perfect math to force a win.
King Usage Uses Kings to block your path, showing you how they restrict movement. Uses Kings to guard the center and avoid being cornered. Keeps Kings mobile to strike from across the board when you least expect it. Masters the “Double Diagonal” and “Long Diagonal” to squeeze your pieces.
“The Move” (Opposition) May explain the concept of having the last move to block you. Counts opposition to ensure it doesn’t get trapped by your King. Willingly loses the “move” if it lures you into a tactical “Shot”. Plays perfectly to win by “Blocking”—leaving you with no legal moves.
Sacrifice Logic Sacrifices only to help you see a specific tactical finish. Sacrifices only if it results in an immediate 1-for-1 trade to simplify. Uses “Decoy-moves”; sacrifices a piece to pull yours into a losing square. Only sacrifices if it triggers a forced sequence that guarantees a win.
Goal of Play To ensure you understand how to use a 2-on-1 King advantage. To provide a fair challenge that tests your basic endgame knowledge. To punish “natural-looking” moves with high-level traps like the “Scorpion Sting”. To convert any small advantage into a win using “First Position” theory.

Endgame Snapshots

  • Teacher: It might put its last piece in a corner to see if you know how to use two Kings to trap it.
  • Balanced: It will trade its last regular piece for yours to reach a standard King-vs-King draw.
  • Trickster: It might leave a piece “unprotected” near the King row. If you jump it, you might find your piece immediately trapped in a “Back Block” or a “619 Trap”.
  • Endgame: It calculates the “opposition” 20 moves ahead to ensure that when only three pieces are left, you are the one forced to move into a capture.
